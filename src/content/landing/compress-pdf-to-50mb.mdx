Compress PDF to Under 50 MB targets the upper end of the file size problem — documents that are not just slightly oversized but genuinely large due to the nature of their content.

Email is the most common trigger. Gmail, Outlook.com, and Yahoo Mail all cap attachments at 25 MB. Files above this must be shared via Drive or Dropbox links — an extra step. Compressing a 60 MB PDF to under 25 MB allows direct email attachment.

WhatsApp's document limit is 100 MB, but files even well under that cause problems on weak mobile signals. A 200 MB PDF may time out repeatedly; a 40 MB version downloads reliably in the same conditions.

File types that regularly reach 100-500 MB include architectural drawings from AutoCAD or Revit, medical imaging PDFs with CT scan or MRI images at diagnostic resolution, large-format flatbed scans at 600 DPI, and print-ready photography portfolios.

For architectural drawings, vector content — dimension lines, annotations, title blocks — is unaffected by compression. Savings come from embedded site photographs and renderings. Text remains sharp at any zoom.

For medical imaging PDFs, compression reduces image resolution. For review and approval workflows, the compressed version is adequate; for diagnostic use, rely on full-resolution PACS images.

If your PDF is too large to load in the browser, split it first and compress each part separately.
