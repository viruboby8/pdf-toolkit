Compress PDF and Preserve Text Quality requires understanding what "quality" actually means in a PDF — and that depends entirely on what the document contains.

A PDF is not a single flat image. It is a container that can hold vector graphics, embedded fonts, typed text, and raster images all at once. Vector text is stored as mathematical descriptions of glyph outlines. When you compress a PDF, vector elements are left completely untouched — they cannot be "degraded" any more than a text file can be blurry. If your PDF is a digital invoice or a typed letter with no photographs, compressing it will barely change its size and will have zero effect on quality.

Raster images are different. Scanned pages, photographs embedded in a document, and screenshots are stored as pixel grids — this is where compression has work to do. PDF Toolkit re-encodes these images using JPEG compression, finding the lowest quality level that still meets your target file size through iterative binary search.

A critical insight: if a PDF is already small, do not compress it further. A 200 KB PDF compressed to 50 KB will likely show visible JPEG artefacts in photographs, though text remains unaffected.

For scanned documents — produced by a smartphone scanner app or an office flatbed — the original scan quality matters. A scan at 300 DPI with good lighting compresses far more gracefully than a dim, low-resolution photo taken at an angle.

The practical test: after compressing, zoom in to 150% in your PDF viewer and check that the text, signature, and any photograph look acceptable. If they do, the file is ready to upload.
