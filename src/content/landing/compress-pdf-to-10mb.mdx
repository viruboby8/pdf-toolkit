Compressing a PDF to under 10 MB addresses the practical realities of email sharing, collaboration tools, and document management systems where very large PDFs cause friction — slow uploads, failed previews, and blocked attachments.

The 10 MB threshold is particularly relevant for:

**Email sharing** — While Gmail technically allows 25 MB attachments, many corporate email gateways, older Outlook configurations, and ISP mail servers reject attachments over 10 MB. Compressing to 10 MB maximises email deliverability without resorting to file-sharing links. For professional correspondence with new clients or government contacts, an email attachment is more reliable than a Google Drive or Dropbox link.

**WhatsApp document sharing** — WhatsApp allows up to 100 MB for documents, but 50 MB PDFs take 30–60 seconds to send over 4G and significantly longer on 3G. A compressed 10 MB file sends in under 5 seconds on most Indian mobile connections and opens instantly on the recipient's device.

**SharePoint and Microsoft Teams** — Teams imposes a 250 MB file limit, but SharePoint administrators frequently configure tighter limits on document libraries used for approvals, HR records, and compliance workflows. 10 MB per document is a common administrator-set cap.

**HRMS and payroll portals** — Platforms like GreytHR, Darwinbox, and Keka impose upload limits on document storage. Compressed salary letters, offer letters, and experience certificates at 10 MB or under are universally accepted.

For multi-page reports and presentations, compressing to 10 MB retains excellent quality — digital-origin PDFs (generated from Word, PowerPoint) compress well because text elements remain as sharp vectors regardless of image compression applied to embedded photos.
