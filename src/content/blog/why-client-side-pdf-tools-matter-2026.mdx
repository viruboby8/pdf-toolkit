Every week, millions of people upload their Aadhaar cards, salary slips, and bank statements to free online PDF compression sites. Most of these sites process files on their servers — which means your sensitive documents travel across the internet, sit on a server you do not control, and may be retained for hours or longer. In 2026, with deepfake-enabled identity fraud at record levels, this is a real risk.

## What happens when you upload to a server-side PDF tool

When you drag a file onto Smallpdf, ILovePDF, or similar services, your PDF is sent over HTTPS to their cloud infrastructure. The file is decrypted, processed, and re-encrypted for download. Most services say they delete files after 1–2 hours. Some retain processed files for longer to power their search or recommendation features.

The practical risk is not that these companies are malicious — most are not. The risk is:

- **Data breaches.** Cloud storage is a high-value target. A stored Aadhaar PDF is personally identifiable information under DPDP Act 2023.
- **Government requests.** Servers have jurisdictions. A GDPR-compliant European company may still respond to a valid government request for data.
- **Third-party sharing.** Free services often share analytics data. A PDF file name like `Aadhaar-Rahul-Sharma-12345.pdf` is metadata that can identify you even if the file content is not read.

## How in-browser PDF processing works

PDF Toolkit processes files entirely inside your browser using WebAssembly (WASM). WASM is a binary instruction format that runs near-native speed in Chrome, Firefox, and Safari without plugins. The JPEG compression engine (mozjpeg), the PDF manipulation library (pdf-lib), and the OCR engine (Tesseract.js) all run as WASM modules inside your browser tab.

You can verify this in 30 seconds: open DevTools → Network tab → compress a PDF. You will see zero outbound requests to any server during processing. The only network activity is the initial page load.

## When you should still use server-side tools

Client-side tools have trade-offs. If you need PDF-to-Word conversion, that requires optical character recognition plus complex layout reconstruction — computationally expensive to run in a browser. E-sign workflows that require a trusted third-party signature authority must be server-side by design.

For the common use cases — compress, merge, split, rotate, convert JPG to PDF, extract text — client-side is both faster and safer. Your file never leaves your device, there is no account to breach, and the tool works offline after the first visit.
